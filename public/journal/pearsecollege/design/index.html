Health and Safety #

  • Planer Thicknesser

    • Keeping the blade cover over most of the blade
    • Using pushstick to move the workpiece
    • Thicknesser
    • Thicknesser needs key to operate.
    • Never putting hands inside the frame of the machine.
    • Hearing protection used at all times.
    • safety glasses.
    • Identifying emergency stop in case it’s needed.
    • Extractor vent is opened with other vents for other machines closed.

  • Bandsaw

    • Hearing protection
    • Goggles
    • Dust extraction
    • Pushstick to keep pieces against the fence and to finish pushing each piece through

  • Domino

    • Hearing protection
    • Goggles
    • Connected to portable dust extractor
    • Hold machine flat against the surface to be dominoed using handles at front and back
    • Using machine left-handed was difficult, much easier to switch on safely while using right hand
    • Took care to tidy cables on floor as there was both power and air lines coming from the portable extractor

  • Drill

    • Make sure pieces are clamped and stable before drilling

  • Mitre saw

    • Used a jig and then clamped pieces to the jig to secure them
    • Hands clear of the blade at all times
    • Let blade stop spinning before moving it from the finishing position
    • Hearing protection
    • Goggles
    • Saw attached to dust extractor

Self-Evaluation #

My original plan needed quite a few updates.

I definitely chose some awkward angles and shapes for the joints. I should have practised before trying them, perhaps figured out some jigs that would have simplified things.

The ash plank had a piece of live edge which could have been avoided in cutting but I didn’t notice it when I marked it up. Since I was using 1 long piece as the back leg I had to incorporate the live egde into the design

My drawings were incomplete. I was making a few changes to my plans and didn’t draw those changes onto new plans. This compounded some of my problems.

There was quite a bit of time pressure as well. I missed a few classes but I was also quite hesitant in putting things together, again having practised some joints and techniques would probably have helped.

I have a poor joint between the back leg and one side of the trangular frame. I filled the gap with (lots of) extra glue so structurally it’s probably fine but it’s definitely very visible.

My front legs are not prefectly square either. Having 3 legs which will always find a stable position was a good choice it seeems.

I still like the design but would rethink my joinery if I was to make it again.
